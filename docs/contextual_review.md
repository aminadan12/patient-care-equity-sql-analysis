# Contextual Review

## Why equity and efficiency are linked
Equity and efficiency in patient care are closely connected. When patients face barriers to access, care becomes less timely and less continuous. This creates inefficiency through missed appointments, delayed treatment, and fragmented care.

## US healthcare context
Existing healthcare research shows that the United States performs poorly compared with peer nations in areas such as healthcare access, efficiency, equity, and outcomes. This project uses that broader context to examine whether similar barriers appear within one hospital system.

## Why delayed access matters
Delays are not only about whether care exists, but whether patients can obtain care in a timely way. Delays may not be evenly distributed, and some demographic groups may face greater financial, logistical, or social barriers that postpone care.

## Why no-shows matter
Missed appointments are often treated as patient non-compliance, but they may also reflect structural barriers. These can include affordability concerns, transport problems, work schedules, caregiving responsibilities, or negative prior experiences with care.

## Why financial friction matters
Insurance coverage does not always make healthcare affordable. Billing and payment patterns can act as signals of financial friction, especially when some groups experience much higher failed payment rates than others.

## Analytical focus
Based on this background, the project focuses on three measurable points where inequity and inefficiency may appear:
1. Delayed access to healthcare
2. Drop-off and disrupted attendance
3. Financial friction in healthcare bills
